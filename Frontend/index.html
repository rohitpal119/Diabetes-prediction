About Diabetes in Women

Diabetes affects women differently than men, with unique risk factors and complications. Understanding these differences is key to prevention and management.

Why Women Are at Risk

Women face unique challenges when it comes to diabetes. Hormonal changes throughout a woman's life—during puberty, pregnancy, and menopause—can affect blood sugar levels and increase diabetes risk.

Gestational diabetes, which occurs during pregnancy, increases a woman's risk of developing type 2 diabetes later in life. Women with diabetes also have a higher risk of heart disease compared to men with diabetes.

Prediction Parameters

Our prediction model uses these key health indicators to assess diabetes risk. Understanding what each parameter means can help you better manage your health.

Pregnancies

The number of times you've been pregnant. Multiple pregnancies can increase diabetes risk.

Glucose Level

Your blood sugar level after fasting. High levels may indicate insulin resistance.

Blood Pressure

Diastolic blood pressure reading. Hypertension is linked to diabetes risk.

Skin Thickness

Triceps skinfold thickness. Measures body fat distribution which affects diabetes risk.

Insulin Level

Blood insulin concentration. High levels may indicate insulin resistance.

BMI

Body Mass Index. Higher BMI is a significant risk factor for type 2 diabetes.

Pedigree Function

Estimates genetic predisposition to diabetes based on family history.

Age

Diabetes risk increases with age, especially after 45 years.
